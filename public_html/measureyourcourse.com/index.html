If you are putting on an event that involves a course on roads or trails, then we have some products and services that will help you put on a quality event, and that will help you build a loyal following for your events.

Course Measurement

We provide you with a certification course map, including a detailed description of the location of the start, finish, splits, and any turnarounds. We also provide a "kml" file for Google Earth that shows the course and the location of the start, finish, splits, and any turnarounds.

We will measure your course so accurately that it will be eligible for certification by USA Track & Field (USATF). You can publish the certification number in your event literature to let prospective participants know that they will be competing on an accurately measured course. With the certification map, your volunteers will be able to reliably setup the course, year after year. We will submit all of the paperwork to get your course certified. The only reason that USATF will not certify your course is if more than 50% of it is on non-paved surfaces. The certification of a course is valid for 10 years.

If you enter enough events, eventually you will encounter one where the mile splits are not in the right place, or where everyone gets a really fast (or slow) time. These are all symptoms of either an inaccurately measured course or a carelessly setup course, or both. Be a race director that cares - get your courses certified.

We use the calibrated bicycle method as the primary measuring technique. After equipping a bicycle with a decimeter accurate odometer, we ride a very carefully measured calibration course four times. We adjust the internal setting of the odometer to the appropriate value, then validate that it is correct by riding the calibration course again. Then, we go to your course and measure it twice. The first time we layout the start, finish, splits and any turnaround, and the second time we validate that they are all in the right place. Then we go back to the calibration course and ride it four more times, just to make sure nothing has changed. Then we permanently mark the location of the start, finish, splits, and any turnaround using surveyor grade PK nails.

We use two backup measuring techniques: Google Earth paths and GPS. Neither is acceptable by USATF as a primary measuring technique, but they do help keep us from making mistakes.

For well defined courses with up-to-date satellite imagery, we construct a Google Earth path that lays out the course and all splits before measuring the course. That way, we know the approximate location of all of the start, finish, splits, and any turnarounds before we get to the course and can verify that we are in the expected place when we are out measuring with the calibrated bike.

We carry a GPS on the bike when we are measuring. GPS is not nearly as accurate as the decimeter accurate odometer, as any point that the GPS records will have an error of at least plus or minus 7 meters. GPS is an excellent approximation tool, however, and we use it to make sure that we have not made a major mistake somewhere.  We track not only the path we are taking, but we also record a waypoint for the start, finish, splits, and any turnaround. We import the GPS information into Google Earth. If we were able to construct a Google Earth path of the course prior to measuring, we compare it with the path from the GPS to see if everything is about the same. If we were not able to construct a Google Earth path, we use the path information from the GPS to build one.

We verify that are notes about the locations of the start, finish, splits and any turnarounds are consistent with Google Earth path.

When everything checks out, we submit the paperwork to USATF to certify the course.

Start/Finish Structures

A start/finish structure is something that holds a banner over the course at a particular point. It is commonly used at the start and at the finish of the course. Two banners can be hung on each structure - one facing the participants as they traverse the course, and the other facing the photographer taking pictures of the faces of participants.

The banner facing the participants is used to indicate to the participants where they are on the course. A large START banner answers a lot of questions prior to race start, and a large FINISH banner provides a clear indication of the goal as participants near the end of the course.

The banner facing the photographer taking pictures of the faces of the participants will be immortalized in a large number of photographs taken at the event - both by spectators and event photographers. This banner gives you an opportunity to advertise your event, or your sponsors, or both.

Our start/finish structures are made from PVC pipe. They are easily transportable in an SUV or the back seat of most 4-door sedans. They are light weight, so almost any volunteer can carry them. One person can assemble and disassemble the structure without tools. Banners are attached to the structure before it is erected, eliminating the need for a ladder. Raising/lowering the structure is most easily done with 3 people. Directions are included.

The standard structure is 12' across (the width of a single lane of traffic or a typical bike path). The banner on the standard structure is 3' high by 12' wide. The bottom edge of the banner is 8' above the ground (high enough that people don't jump up and try to hit it when they run under it). Custom widths up to 26' have been constructed and used.

There are at least two types of banner material that work well -- solid vinyl and mesh. While mesh banners are slightly more expensive, we recommend them because they significantly reduce the wind load on the structure. In May of 2009, we erected a finish structure for the We Walk! Marathon & Half on a day with 25 mph sustained winds. The structure and banner stayed up for 6 hours, enduring gusts up to 35 mph.

Signs & Mile Markers

There is always stuff along a course that you want to tell the participants about. Our signs are easy to setup, easy to read,  sturdy enough to handle wind/rain/snow and reusable.

Our signs are printed on an 11" high by 8 1/2" wide brightly colored cardstock which is then laminated to add stiffness and water proofing. We can print almost anything on the sign. In addition to mile markers, km markers and course direction arrows, we can provide where-to-go signs for registration, packet pickup, bathrooms, the start, etc. We can provide two-sided signs (especially useful for the where-to-go signs).

The sign is then affixed to a 3' tall plastic fence post with a metal spike in the bottom. On days when the ground is not frozen, you just push the spike into the ground. When the ground is frozen, take a battery operated drill and drill a 3/8" hole into the ground and then insert the spike. When there is no dirt around to push the spike into the plastic fence post that we use is not an ideal solution. (Though you can duct tape or tie the post to something solid. We've used weighted traffic cones, city sign posts, parking meter posts and telephone poles to hold signs. Please don't use duct tape on a car!)

The 3' tall post provides adequate visibility for low density environments where people are moving (for example, on a 5k race course with 250 people). When the density of people is high or they are standing around (for example, at packet pickup in a 5k race with 1000 people), you have to put the sign above head height, so the people in front do not block the sign.

Race Day Course Management

Setting up and taking down a course on race day is something that is most easily done with an experienced person in charge. If you can provide a couple of volunteers and the necessary traffic cones, we can provide an experienced person to handle course setup and taking down a course.
